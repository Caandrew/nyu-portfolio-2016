THE IVAR IKEA INTERVENTION

Are we to hang our hearts on such little things” Yes, and without hesitation.
-Sigmund Freud

This Freudian mindset permeates IKEA throughout the retail giant, but there is one major difference: Freud was, I think, talking about possessions that have permanence, that are carefully chosen to become a part of peoples life stories. IKEA however, does the exact opposite of that. Rather than placing importance on individuality, the company promotes sameness and perpetuates a culture of disposability; selling brand new furniture that, to quote Hellmuth Karasek, is junk.

Reflecting on my own purchases from superstore I realized that, during my years as an undergrad, I wasted an inordinate amount of both money and time on purchases that have no permanence and, respectively, spent a significant amount time in IKEA’s intentionally labyrinth-like stores — designed to immerse patrons and encourage them to make purchase that “open [their] wallets” by placing items that elicit the mindset that these especially disposable items are “so affordable that [shoppers] can’t afford not to buy them.”

The last purchases I made from IKEA were in were in 2012, when I moved to Seattle with my then Partner. We walked through the winding paths in their Redond location, picking and choosing stuff that would furnish our new life together. Much like that relationship, those things, save for one laundry hamper, are no longer a part of my life.

Don’t get me wrong, I love stuff and things. My room is full of baubles and the like that have moved with me from Arizona to Seattle and then to New York, because I am so attached to them. The only reason I still have that laundry hamper is because I used it to pack those items of great importance during my last move.
